\section{Introduction}

Machine-learning models are increasingly trained and deployed through
outsourced and cloud-based pipelines. Machine learning as a service
(MLaaS) allows clients with limited computational resources or machine-learning
expertise to outsource training and inference tasks to external providers
\cite{MLaaS15, VeriML21, privacy-mlaas23}. This paradigm is attractive in
settings such as risk scoring, healthcare analytics, fraud detection, and
financial decision making, where predictive models are often trained or served
over sensitive tabular data \cite{xgboost16,grinsztajn2022tree,shwartz2022tabular}.
However, outsourcing also creates an integrity problem: a client may receive a
trained model or a prediction from an external provider, but
cannot directly tell whether it was produced by the claimed training pipeline on
the claimed data source. Prior work on verifiable MLaaS and proof-of-learning
has therefore emphasized the need to certify that outsourced training or
inference was executed correctly, rather than replaced by a shortcut, a
partially trained model, or an improperly executed computation
\cite{VeriML21,proof_of_learning21,zkdnn24}.

At the same time, full transparency is often unacceptable. Training data may
contain sensitive user, medical, or financial records, while the learned model
itself may encode proprietary information or leak information about the
training data \cite{privacy-mlaas23,tramer2016stealing}. Model extraction
attacks further show that even black-box prediction APIs can compromise model
confidentiality, especially for models offered through cloud prediction
services \cite{tramer2016stealing}. This creates a tension between
verifiability and confidentiality: the verifier wants assurance that the model
was trained correctly, whereas the prover may need to keep the data, model
parameters, and intermediate training states private. Zero-knowledge proofs of
training address this tension by allowing a prover to convince a verifier that a
committed model is the output of a public training algorithm on a committed
dataset, while revealing nothing beyond the validity of this statement
\cite{zkdnn24,zkml_survey}.

In this work, we study zero-knowledge proofs for gradient boosted decision
trees (GBDTs). GBDTs, and especially implementations such as XGBoost~\cite{xgboost16}, are among
the strongest model families for tabular data and are widely used in domains
where accountability matters~\cite{shwartz2022tabular, grinsztajn2022tree}. A GBDT model is an additive ensemble of decision
trees: each round trains new trees to fit the residual errors of the current
model, and the final prediction is obtained by aggregating the outputs of all
trees. This sequential structure makes training certification substantially
harder than inference certification. To prove inference, it is enough to show
that each input follows a valid path in each committed tree and that the
returned scores are aggregated correctly. To prove training, the prover must
also show that every split, every leaf value, and every boosting update is
consistent with the committed dataset and the public training rule.

Prior zero-knowledge works on tree-based models have mostly focused on
inference, accuracy testing, or auditing properties of already trained trees.
zkDT~\cite{zkdt20} proves decision-tree predictions and accuracy claims without
revealing the tree. Later works improve the representation of committed trees
and use lookup arguments to make tree inference more efficient
\cite{cq++24}. Confidential-PROFITT~\cite{confidential-profitt22} targets a
different goal: it certifies fairness properties of decision-tree training while
keeping both the model and training data confidential. These works provide
important tools for tree-model verification, but they do not give a specialized
proof system for the full training semantics of gradient boosting.

The closest line of work is zero-knowledge proof of training for tree
ensembles. Sparrow~\cite{sparrow24} gives a space-efficient zkSNARK for
data-parallel circuits and applies it to random-forest training and prediction.
Its key idea is to avoid re-running training inside the proof: instead, the
prover proves that a certification algorithm accepts the committed forest and
dataset. ZKBoost~\cite{zkboost26} extends this certification approach to XGBoost.
It defines a deterministic fixed-point version of XGBoost, builds a
certification algorithm for the resulting trainer, and then proves this
certifier using a general-purpose ZKP backend. These works demonstrate that
proofs of training for tree ensembles are possible, but their proof systems
still inherit the cost of proving a large certification computation. In
particular, constructing histograms, validating split optimality, checking
fixed-point arithmetic, and handling RAM-style updates become dominant costs.

A natural alternative is to avoid proving the certification computation as a
generic circuit and instead prove the algebraic relations that characterize
GBDT training directly. However, this approach creates a different
bottleneck. GBDT training contains many repeated constraints over many axes:
rounds, classes, nodes, features, bins, and samples. Some constraints are linear
relations, such as score updates, prefix sums, and aggregation equations. Others
are local nonlinear relations, such as lookup checks, range checks,
quantization, division, and maximum selection. Moreover, these constraints do
not naturally live on one uniform domain. Different tensors have different
shapes, and their polynomial encodings are committed over different subgroups. Proving each relation separately would introduce a large number of
auxiliary commitments and openings, and ordinary same-domain batching does not
apply directly.

We present \textsc{Terrae}, a zero-knowledge proof system for quantized GBDT
training and inference based on KZG polynomial commitments. \textsc{Terrae}
proves that a committed forest is obtained by executing a public quantized GBDT
trainer on a committed dataset. Instead of compiling the whole certifier into a
generic arithmetic circuit, \textsc{Terrae} represents the training trace as
committed tensors and proves the relations among them directly. The proof
follows the structure of histogram-based tree training: it proves that samples
are routed to the correct leaves, that sample-wise residuals are correctly
converted into per-node and per-bin histograms, that the selected split at each
internal node is optimal according to the public splitting rule, that leaf
values are computed from the committed residuals, and that boosting states are
updated consistently across rounds.

The main technical challenge is batching. Once the training semantics are
decomposed into algebraic relations, the prover faces many similar constraints
over different domains. \textsc{Terrae} introduces two batching tools to handle
this heterogeneity. First, \emph{domain-lifting batching} combines linear
zero-check and sum-check claims whose natural domains have different sizes. It
lifts all residual polynomials to one same domain and then applies a
single random linear combination. Second, \emph{interleaving batching} combines
local nonlinear checks, including lookup-style checks, by virtually
interleaving committed vectors of different lengths. The resulting batched
object is assembled from existing commitments and public sparse selectors, so
the batching does not require new commitments to the merged vectors.

We also design a dedicated histogram proof for GBDT training. Prior
certification-based approaches treat histogram construction as a RAM-like
update procedure~\cite{sparrow24,zkboost26}: each sample updates the bucket indexed by its leaf, feature,
and bin, by reading the value from and writing the updated value back to the same location.
In contrast, \textsc{Terrae} exploits the fact that histogram updates
are fixed additive updates. It proves the final histogram by binding each bucket
to a power of random challenge and checking a weighted-sum identity between the committed
histogram and the sample-wise update stream. This avoids proving a general RAM
computation and gives a proof primitive tailored to the histogram structure
that appears throughout tree training.

Our main contributions are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We formulate zero-knowledge proofs for quantized GBDT training and
    inference over KZG commitments. The proved statement binds a committed
    forest to a committed dataset and a public training algorithm, while keeping
    the dataset, model, and intermediate training trace private when hiding
    commitments are used.

    \item We give specialized algebraic protocols for the main components of
    GBDT training: sample routing, histogram construction, split-score
    computation, split optimality, leaf-value computation, and boosting-state
    updates. These protocols avoid compiling the entire training certifier into
    a generic arithmetic circuit.

    \item We introduce domain-lifting batching for linear constraints and
    interleaving batching for local nonlinear constraints. These techniques
    batch constraints that arise over differently sized domains and reduce many
    commitment-opening tasks to a small number of batched claims, without
    committing to extra witnesses.

    \item We propose a histogram proof that proves the correctness of converting
    sample-wise training data and residuals into per-node, per-feature, and
    per-bin histograms. The proof exploits the additive structure of histogram
    construction and avoids general RAM consistency checking.

    \item We implement \textsc{Terrae} and evaluate it against a
    certification-based baseline following the design of prior proof-of-training
    systems for tree ensembles. The results show that directly proving the
    algebraic structure of GBDT training significantly reduces proof-generation
    time, while adding only modest proof-size overhead.
\end{itemize}
