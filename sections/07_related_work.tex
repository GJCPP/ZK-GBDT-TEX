\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Zero-knowledge proofs for tree models.}
Early work on zero-knowledge proofs for tree inference focused on certifying
predictions of decision trees and random forests without exposing the model
\cite{zkdt20}. CQ++ \cite{cq++24} showed how to encode decision trees with
hyperboxes and verify inference with lookup arguments over KZG commitments.
Sparrow \cite{sparrow24} demonstrated that data-parallel zkSNARK techniques can
also be effective for tree workloads. Our construction starts from the same
high-level observation as these systems, namely that tree-specific structure
should remain visible to the prover, but it targets a different bottleneck:
proving GBDT training, and in particular proving optimal split selection in a
way that scales with histogram size rather than with a large generic circuit.

\paragraph{Lookup arguments and algebraic proof components.}
At the proof-system layer, our construction uses KZG commitments \cite{kzg10},
Aurora-style sum-checks \cite{aurora19}, and lookup protocols based on cached
quotients and matrix lookup \cite{cq22,cq++24,baloo25}. These ingredients are
not new individually. What is new here is the way they are assembled around a
common batched zero-check template and a small primitive catalog tailored to
GBDT training, with aggregation and histogram construction promoted from
implementation details to explicit relations in the statement of the protocol.

\paragraph{zkML inference and training.}
The pseudo-residual part of our protocol is also related to the broader zkML
literature on proving nonlinear inference and training updates. zkCNN
\cite{zkcnn21} illustrates how to certify prediction-time neural computations,
while Kaizen \cite{zkdnn24} studies zero-knowledge proofs of neural-network
training. More recently, ZKBoost \cite{zkboost26} considers verifiable training
for XGBoost. Our focus is different from these generic or semi-generic training
systems: we isolate the tree-specific combinatorial core of GBDT training and
optimize that layer directly, rather than compiling split search into a
monolithic backend.
