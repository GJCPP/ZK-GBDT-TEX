\section{Batched Proof Tools}\label{sec:tools}

This section states the two batching tools used repeatedly in the protocol. The
first handles heterogeneous linear identities that initially live on different
polynomial domains. The second handles many same-form nonlinear checks after
their witness bundles have been aligned to a common shape. Together they cover
the two batching patterns that recur throughout the rest of the construction.
Standard divisibility-based zero checks and same-domain batching already appear
in prior proof systems such as Aurora and CQ++ \cite{aurora19,cq++24}. Our
contribution here is to isolate two stronger reusable tools: one for lifting
heterogeneous-domain linear claims before batching, and one for interleaving
aligned same-form nonlinear claims into a single pointwise relation.

\subsection{Domain-Lifted Batch}

To the best of our knowledge, domain-lifted batch is new as a reusable
heterogeneous-domain batching theorem in this line of KZG-based protocol
design. We use divisibility claims because a row-wise identity over
$\mathbb{H}_n$ is equivalent to the statement that its residual polynomial
vanishes on every point of $\mathbb{H}_n$, and therefore is divisible by the
vanishing polynomial $\nu_n(X)$. The theorem below formalizes how such claims
can be lifted to a common larger domain before batching.

\begin{theorem}[Domain-lifted batch]
\label{thm:domain-lifted-batch}
Let
\[
1 \leq n_1 < n_2 < \cdots < n_m = N
\]
be powers of two. For each $i \in [m]$, let $F_i(X)$ be a virtual polynomial
and suppose the target claim is
\[
F_i(X)=Q_i(X)\nu_{n_i}(X)
\]
for some polynomial $Q_i(X)$. Define the lifted residual
\[
\widehat{F}_i(X):=
F_i(X)\frac{\nu_N(X)}{\nu_{n_i}(X)}.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $F_i(X)=Q_i(X)\nu_{n_i}(X)$ holds if and only if
  $\widehat{F}_i(X)$ is divisible by $\nu_N(X)$.
  \item For a verifier challenge $\eta$, all $m$ claims can be combined into
  the single identity
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:domain-lifted-batch}
  \sum_{i=1}^{m}\eta^{i-1}\widehat{F}_i(X)=Q(X)\nu_N(X).
  \end{equation}
  \item If at least one original claim is false, then
  \eqref{eq:domain-lifted-batch} holds for at most $m-1$ values of $\eta$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first item is immediate from
\[
F_i(X)=Q_i(X)\nu_{n_i}(X)
\iff
F_i(X)\frac{\nu_N(X)}{\nu_{n_i}(X)} = Q_i(X)\nu_N(X).
\]
For soundness, once the lifted residuals are reduced modulo $\nu_N(X)$, the
left-hand side of \eqref{eq:domain-lifted-batch} becomes a polynomial in $\eta$
of degree at most $m-1$. If at least one original claim is false, this
polynomial is nonzero, so a uniformly random $\eta$ causes accidental
acceptance with probability at most $(m-1)/|\mathbb{F}|$.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:domain-lifted-batch} is the protocol's main tool for batching
heterogeneous linear identities. Later we apply it to routing equations,
padding-induced tensor equalities, and other zero-check-style relations whose
natural domains are not identical.

\subsection{Interleaved Batch}

To the best of our knowledge, interleaved batch is likewise new as a reusable
way to collapse many same-form nonlinear gadget instances into one larger
virtual instance. The intended setting is that many checks share the same local
rule and the same public parameters, but arrive as separate witness bundles on
possibly different power-of-two domains. We first record the alignment lemma
used to place smaller objects on larger domains.

\begin{lemma}[Row replication]
\label{lem:row-replication}
Let $\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{F}^m$ and let $v(X)$ be its encoding over
$\mathbb{H}_m$. Let $\mathbf{V}\in\mathbb{F}^{r\times m}$ be the matrix whose
every row equals $\mathbf{v}$. Then the encoding polynomial of $\mathbf{V}$ over
$\mathbb{H}_{rm}$ is
\[
V(X)=v(X^r).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For every $i\in[r]$ and $j\in[m]$,
\[
V(\omega_{rm}^{im+j})
= \mathbf{V}[i][j]
= \mathbf{v}[j]
= v(\omega_m^j)
= v((\omega_{rm}^{im+j})^r),
\]
where the last equality uses $\omega_{rm}^r=\omega_m$. Since both sides have
degree less than $rm$ and agree on $\mathbb{H}_{rm}$, they are identical.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Pairwise interleaving]
\label{def:pairwise-interleaving}
Let $f(X)$ and $g(X)$ be the encoding polynomials of two length-$n$ vectors
over $\mathbb{H}_n$, where $n$ is a power of two. Define
\[
\operatorname{even}_n(X):=\frac{X^n+1}{2},
\qquad
\operatorname{odd}_n(X):=\frac{1-X^n}{2}.
\]
Their evaluations on $\mathbb{H}_{2n}$ are the alternating selectors
$(1,0,1,0,\ldots)$ and $(0,1,0,1,\ldots)$. The pairwise interleaving of
$f(X)$ and $g(X)$ is the virtual polynomial
\[
\operatorname{Int}_n(f,g)(X)
:=
\operatorname{even}_n(X)\,f(X)
+
\operatorname{odd}_n(X)\,g(\omega_{2n}^{-1}X).
\]
Its evaluation vector over $\mathbb{H}_{2n}$ is
\[
(f[0],g[0],f[1],g[1],\ldots,f[n-1],g[n-1]).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Interleaving batch]
\label{thm:interleaving-batch}
For each $i\in[m]$, let $u_i(X)$ denote the full witness bundle of one gadget
instance over $\mathbb{H}_{n_i}$, where each $n_i$ is a power of two. We write
$u_i(a)$ for the local tuple extracted from that bundle at
$a\in\mathbb{H}_{n_i}$. After grouping together the instances that share the
same public data, assume every point must satisfy one common local relation
\[
\varphi\!\left(
u_i(a)
\right)=0
\qquad
\forall a\in\mathbb{H}_{n_i}.
\]
Maintain a multiset of active pairs, initially
$\{(u_i(X),n_i)\}_{i\in[m]}$, and recursively apply the following merge
procedure.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item If two active pairs have the same domain size $n$, replace them by one
  active pair of domain size $2n$ obtained by pairwise interleaving their
  corresponding components as in \cref{def:pairwise-interleaving}.
  \item Otherwise, let $n_i<n_j$ be the two smallest active domain sizes. Set
  $r=n_j/n_i$. Replace $(u_i(X),n_i)$ by $(u_i(X^r),n_j)$.
  \item Repeat until one active pair
  $(\widehat{u}(X),\widehat{n})$
  remains.
\end{enumerate}
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item all original pointwise claims hold if and only if
  \[
  \varphi\!\left(
  \widehat{u}(z)
  \right)=0
  \qquad
  \forall z\in\mathbb{H}_{\widehat{n}};
  \]
  \item the final domain size satisfies
  \[
  \widehat{n}
  =
  2^{\left\lceil \log_2(n_1+\cdots+n_m)\right\rceil}.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For the first claim, the lifting step is exactly the setting of
\cref{lem:row-replication}: if $r=n_j/n_i$, then the substitution
$X\mapsto X^r$ repeats the entries of the evaluation vector of $u_i(X)$
periodically on the larger domain $\mathbb{H}_{n_j}$. Therefore the pointwise
relation $\varphi(\cdot)=0$ holds
before lifting if and only if it holds after lifting. Once two active pairs
share one domain, pairwise interleaving places one tuple on the even positions
and the other on the odd positions of the doubled domain. Thus the merged pair
satisfies the same pointwise relation if and only if both original pairs do.
Recursing proves the first item.

For the second claim, each merge step replaces two power-of-two domain sizes
$a\leq b$ by one new size $2b$. This is exactly
$2^{\lceil \log_2(a+b)\rceil}$: if $a=b$, the claim is immediate; if $a<b$,
then $a\leq b/2$, so
\[
b < a+b \leq \frac{3b}{2} < 2b,
\]
and therefore
\[
2^{\lceil \log_2(a+b)\rceil}=2b.
\]

We now prove by induction on the number of active pairs that the final domain
size is $2^{\lceil \log_2(\sum_i n_i)\rceil}$. The base case $m=1$ is trivial.
For the induction step, let $a\leq b$ be the two sizes merged first, and let
$R$ be the sum of all remaining sizes. Because the algorithm chooses the two
smallest active domains, every remaining size is a power of two at least $b$,
so $R$ is a multiple of $b$. Let
\[
c:=2^{\lceil \log_2(a+b)\rceil}=2b.
\]
After the first merge, the algorithm continues on a reduced multiset whose
total size is $R+c$. By the induction hypothesis, the final size is
$2^{\lceil \log_2(R+c)\rceil}$. It remains to show that
\[
2^{\lceil \log_2(R+c)\rceil}
=
2^{\lceil \log_2(R+a+b)\rceil}.
\]
Write $R=kb$ with $k\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$. Since $b$ is itself a power of two,
\[
2^{\lceil \log_2(bx)\rceil}
=
b\cdot 2^{\lceil \log_2 x\rceil}
\]
for every positive real $x$. Therefore it suffices to compare
\[
x:=k+1+\frac{a}{b}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
y:=k+2.
\]
We have $x\in(y-1,y]$. If $y$ is not a power of two, then no power of two lies
in the interval $(y-1,y]$, so
\[
2^{\lceil \log_2 x\rceil}=2^{\lceil \log_2 y\rceil}.
\]
If $y=2^t$, then $x>2^t-1\ge 2^{t-1}$, hence both $x$ and $y$ lie in the same
dyadic interval $(2^{t-1},2^t]$ and again have the same power-of-two ceiling.
Thus
\[
2^{\lceil \log_2(R+c)\rceil}
=
2^{\lceil \log_2(R+a+b)\rceil},
\]
which completes the induction.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:interleaving-batch} is useful when the protocol produces many
small nonlinear checks of the same form. Examples include several lookup-style
table-difference checks, several same-form rounding relations of the form
$n=qd+r$ with bounded remainder, and other small gadgets that share one local
rule together with one public parameter tuple. Rather than assign a separate
batch to each local instance, the prover recursively lifts and interleaves them
into one larger virtual bundle and proves one resulting pointwise relation. The
merged bundle is still virtual: every component is assembled from existing
commitments by public sparse selectors and domain-lift substitutions, so no new
base commitment is required. In concrete protocols, we first group checks by
their shared public parameters, such as a common lookup table or a common
divisor, and then apply the theorem within each group.
